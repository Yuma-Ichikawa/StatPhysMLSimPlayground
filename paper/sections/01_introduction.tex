\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Transformer capabilities often change rapidly as data, scale, or training
time is varied.  Calling every rapid change a phase transition is not useful:
a genuine statistical-mechanics claim requires an order parameter, a declared
large-size path, fluctuation measurements, and finite-size evidence.  At the
other extreme, studying only production-scale language models makes those
requirements difficult to satisfy because architecture, data, optimization,
and evaluation all change together.  The productive middle ground is a
controlled teacher--student system that retains an identifiable Transformer
mechanism while permitting dense, replicated numerical experiments.

Cui et al. supplied such a starting point by deriving a positional--semantic
transition in a two-token attention model with a tied low-rank query/key
operator \citep{cui2024phase}.  The model is small enough for asymptotic
analysis yet already contains a nontrivial competition: the student can use a
fixed positional displacement or recover a latent semantic direction.  The
transition is therefore a rare bridge between an exactly characterized
attention mechanism and a question that matters for realistic Transformers.
It is not, however, evidence that the same phase structure holds after Q/K
untieing, full-width projections, multiple heads, residual streams,
normalization, MLPs, causality, rotary position encoding, depth, or
autoregressive training.

We ask a sharply scoped question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Which statistical signatures of the positional--semantic transition
survive controlled movement from the solvable attention model toward a
Transformer, and along which thermodynamic scaling path?}
\end{quote}
The point is not to declare that ``LLMs are thermodynamic.''  It is to test a
specific mechanism under a sequence of falsifiable interventions.

Our design is a two-dimensional ladder.  The architecture ladder $M0$--$M8$
adds one family of mechanisms at a time.  The data ladder $D0$--$D5$ holds the
teacher task fixed while changing the token ensemble from Gaussian to
correlated, heavy-tailed, discrete, stateful, and grammatical.  Each cell is
evaluated along explicitly recorded scaling paths such as
$n=c d^\gamma$, fixed or growing head dimension, fixed or growing number of
heads, and fixed or growing depth.  We make no universality claim by comparing
models at a single width.

The central methodological choice is to measure the learned \emph{function},
not only a weight cosine.  On a shared probe set we regress the student's
centered output onto pure positional and semantic teacher functions, yielding
partial functional order parameters $(\mpos,\msem)$ and a residual fraction.
This remains meaningful when heads can permute and layers can reparameterize
the same computation.  We complement it with head--latent assignment,
effective QK/OV spectra, representation participation ratios, causal
interventions, and the thermodynamic fluctuation statistics needed to
separate a crossover from a transition candidate.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item a faithful, tested reproduction layer for the exact
  positional--semantic bridge, including the original normalization and
  summed empirical loss;
  \item orthogonal $M0$--$M8$ architecture and $D0$--$D5$ data ladders behind
  a common instrumented interface;
  \item a conservative transition protocol combining hierarchical
  replication, susceptibilities, Binder crossings, rate functions,
  hysteresis, and finite-size collapse;
  \item a portable Slurm implementation in which manifests, seeds,
  provenance, checksums, failures, and claim eligibility are machine-audited;
  and
  \item a claim registry that specifies in advance what would support or
  falsify each proposed mechanism.
\end{enumerate}
